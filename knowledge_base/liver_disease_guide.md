# Liver Disease: Complete Health Guide

## Overview
The liver is the body's largest internal organ and is responsible for over 500 vital functions including detoxification, protein synthesis, bile production, and metabolism of carbohydrates, fats, and proteins. Liver disease affects more than 1.5 billion people worldwide and is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality. Most liver conditions are silent until significant damage has occurred, making screening and early intervention critical.

## Types of Liver Disease

### Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) / NASH
- **NAFLD**: Fat accumulation in liver cells (steatosis) without significant alcohol use; affects ~25% of global population
- **NASH (Non-Alcoholic Steatohepatitis)**: Inflammation and injury added to steatosis; progresses to fibrosis/cirrhosis in 10-25%
- Strongest associations: obesity (80%), Type 2 diabetes, metabolic syndrome, dyslipidemia
- Now renamed MASLD (Metabolic dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease) in newer guidelines

### Alcoholic Liver Disease (ALD)
- **Alcoholic fatty liver**: Reversible with abstinence; occurs after even short periods of heavy drinking
- **Alcoholic hepatitis**: Acute inflammation; can be severe with high short-term mortality
- **Alcoholic cirrhosis**: End-stage; develops after years of heavy drinking (>60g/day men, >20g/day women)

### Viral Hepatitis
- **Hepatitis A (HAV)**: Fecal-oral transmission; self-limited; vaccine available; no chronicity
- **Hepatitis B (HBV)**: Blood/sexual transmission; 5-10% become chronic; risk of cirrhosis and HCC; vaccine available
- **Hepatitis C (HCV)**: Blood transmission; 70-80% become chronic; curable with direct-acting antivirals (DAAs); no vaccine
- **Hepatitis D**: Only with HBV coinfection; more severe disease
- **Hepatitis E**: Fecal-oral; usually self-limited; dangerous in pregnancy

### Cirrhosis
- Final common pathway of chronic liver injury; replacement of normal liver tissue with scar (fibrotic) tissue
- Compensated: No major complications; may be asymptomatic
- Decompensated: Ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, jaundice, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP)
- Child-Pugh and MELD scores assess severity

### Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)
- Most common primary liver cancer; 90% occur in cirrhotic livers
- Major risk factors: HBV, HCV, cirrhosis from any cause, aflatoxin exposure
- Screening: Ultrasound +/- AFP every 6 months for high-risk patients

### Autoimmune Liver Diseases
- **Autoimmune hepatitis**: Immune attack on liver cells; treated with immunosuppressants
- **Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)**: Destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts; elevated ALP, anti-mitochondrial antibodies
- **Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)**: Inflammation and fibrosis of bile ducts; associated with IBD

### Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI)
- Most common cause: acetaminophen (paracetamol) overdose
- Other offenders: isoniazid, amoxicillin-clavulanate, statins, herbal supplements
- Can mimic any pattern of liver disease

## Risk Factors
- Obesity and metabolic syndrome (for NAFLD/NASH)
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Viral hepatitis B or C infection
- Unprotected sexual contact; sharing needles/blood exposure
- Family history of liver disease or genetic disorders (hemochromatosis, Wilson's disease, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency)
- Diabetes mellitus Type 2
- Exposure to environmental toxins (aflatoxins, industrial chemicals)
- Use of certain medications or herbal supplements without medical supervision

## Symptoms

### Early/Mild Disease (often asymptomatic)
- Often no symptoms; discovered incidentally on lab work
- Mild right upper quadrant discomfort or fullness
- Elevated liver enzymes on routine bloodwork without symptoms

### Moderate Disease
- Nausea, vomiting, decreased appetite
- Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes) from bilirubin buildup
- Dark urine (bilirubinuria) and pale/clay-colored stools (cholestasis)
- Pruritus (itching) from bile salt accumulation
- Fatigue and weakness

### Advanced/Severe Disease (Decompensated Cirrhosis)
- Ascites: Fluid accumulation in the abdomen causing distension
- Variceal bleeding: Vomiting blood or passing black tarry stools (melena) - medical emergency
- Hepatic encephalopathy: Confusion, asterixis (flapping tremor), disorientation, coma
- Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): Fever, abdominal pain, worsening encephalopathy
- Spider angiomas: Spider-like blood vessel clusters on skin
- Palmar erythema: Redness of palms
- Caput medusae: Dilated abdominal veins
- Gynecomastia in men, loss of body hair

## Liver Function Tests (LFTs) and Key Lab Values

### Hepatocellular Injury Markers
| Test | Normal Range | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) | 7-56 U/L | Most liver-specific; elevated in hepatitis, NAFLD |
| AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase) | 10-40 U/L | Less specific; AST:ALT >2 suggests alcoholic disease |
| LDH | 122-222 U/L | Non-specific; very high in ischemic hepatitis |

### Cholestatic Markers
| Test | Normal Range | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase) | 44-147 U/L | Elevated in biliary obstruction, PBC, PSC |
| GGT (Gamma-GT) | 9-48 U/L | Sensitive for alcohol use; elevated with ALP suggests bile duct disease |
| Total Bilirubin | 0.2-1.2 mg/dL | Elevated = jaundice; >2.5 mg/dL clinically visible |
| Direct (Conjugated) Bilirubin | 0-0.3 mg/dL | Elevated in cholestasis, hepatitis |

### Synthetic Function
| Test | Normal Range | Significance |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| Albumin | 3.5-5.0 g/dL | Low in chronic liver disease; poor prognosis if <3.5 |
| Prothrombin Time (PT/INR) | INR 0.8-1.2 | Prolonged in advanced disease; liver synthesizes clotting factors |
| Total Protein | 6.0-8.3 g/dL | Reflects albumin + globulins |

### Fibrosis and Special Tests
- **Platelet count**: Often low in cirrhosis due to portal hypertension and splenic sequestration
- **AFP (Alpha-fetoprotein)**: Elevated in HCC (>200 ng/mL); also elevated in hepatitis flares
- **FIB-4 Score**: (Age x AST) / (Platelets x sqrt(ALT)); >2.67 suggests advanced fibrosis
- **Liver biopsy**: Gold standard for staging fibrosis/cirrhosis (METAVIR F0-F4 scale)
- **FibroScan (Transient Elastography)**: Non-invasive liver stiffness measurement

## Scoring Systems
- **Child-Pugh Score** (A=5-6, B=7-9, C=10-15): Uses bilirubin, albumin, INR, ascites, encephalopathy
- **MELD Score**: Predicts 90-day mortality; used for transplant organ allocation

## Treatment

### Lifestyle Modifications
- Weight loss 5-10% of body weight significantly reduces liver fat and inflammation in NAFLD/NASH
- Complete alcohol abstinence for alcoholic liver disease
- Regular exercise: 150+ min/week moderate activity reduces hepatic steatosis
- Healthy diet: Mediterranean diet; reduce fructose, refined carbohydrates, red meat
- Avoid hepatotoxic medications and herbal supplements without physician guidance
- Vaccinate against hepatitis A and B if not immune

### Medications
| Condition | Treatment |
|-----------|-----------|
| Hepatitis C | Direct-acting antivirals (SOF/VEL, GLE/PIB): >95% cure rate in 8-12 weeks |
| Hepatitis B | Tenofovir, Entecavir (viral suppression, not cure) |
| Autoimmune Hepatitis | Prednisone + Azathioprine |
| PBC | Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA), Obeticholic acid |
| NAFLD/NASH | Semaglutide, Resmetirom (FDA-approved for NASH with fibrosis) |
| Ascites | Salt restriction + Spironolactone +/- Furosemide |
| Hepatic Encephalopathy | Lactulose, Rifaximin (reduce gut ammonia) |
| SBP | Cefotaxime IV; albumin infusion reduces mortality |
| HCC | Resection, ablation, TACE, sorafenib/lenvatinib, immunotherapy |

### Liver Transplantation
- Indicated for decompensated cirrhosis (MELD 15+), HCC within Milan criteria, fulminant hepatic failure
- 5-year survival rate: ~75-80%
- Living-donor or deceased-donor transplant

## Monitoring and Follow-Up
- Cirrhotic patients: HCC surveillance with ultrasound +/- AFP every 6 months
- Varices screening: Upper endoscopy at diagnosis of cirrhosis, repeat every 1-3 years
- Vaccinations: Hepatitis A, B, pneumococcal, influenza
- Avoid NSAIDs, sedatives, narcotics in advanced liver disease

## When to Seek Medical Attention
- Sudden severe abdominal pain or marked abdominal distension
- Vomiting blood or black/tarry stools (variceal bleed - call emergency services immediately)
- Sudden confusion, disorientation, or unresponsiveness (hepatic encephalopathy)
- Fever with abdominal tenderness in a patient with ascites (possible SBP)
- New or worsening jaundice
- Significant unintentional weight loss
- Discovered liver mass on imaging
