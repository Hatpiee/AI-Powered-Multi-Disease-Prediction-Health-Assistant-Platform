# Kidney (Renal) Disease: Complete Health Guide

## Overview
The kidneys are two bean-shaped organs that filter approximately 200 liters of blood per day, removing waste products and excess fluid as urine. They also regulate blood pressure, produce erythropoietin (stimulates red blood cell production), activate vitamin D, and maintain electrolyte balance. Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD) affects approximately 850 million people worldwide and is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease and premature death. CKD progresses silently, often reaching advanced stages before symptoms appear.

## Types of Kidney Disease

### Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
- Structural or functional abnormality of the kidney lasting 3+ months
- Progressive loss of kidney function over months to years
- Primary causes: diabetic nephropathy (most common, 40%), hypertensive nephrosclerosis, glomerulonephritis

### Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)
- Sudden decline in kidney function over hours to days
- Causes: Pre-renal (dehydration, heart failure), intrinsic renal (glomerulonephritis, ATN), post-renal (obstruction)
- Reversible if caught and treated early; can transition to CKD if severe or prolonged

### Diabetic Nephropathy
- Leading cause of CKD and ESRD worldwide
- Occurs in ~40% of people with diabetes; more common with poor glycemic control
- Pathology: glomerular hyperfiltration to microalbuminuria to macroalbuminuria to declining eGFR to ESRD
- Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules) on biopsy

### Hypertensive Nephrosclerosis
- Chronic hypertension damages small renal vessels causing ischemic injury to glomeruli
- Second most common cause of ESRD
- Benign nephrosclerosis (slow progression) vs malignant nephrosclerosis (rapid progression)

### Glomerulonephritis
- Inflammation of the glomeruli (filtering units)
- Nephrotic syndrome: heavy proteinuria (>3.5g/day), hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia
- Nephritic syndrome: hematuria, hypertension, oliguria, mild proteinuria
- Causes: IgA nephropathy (most common worldwide), membranous nephropathy, focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), lupus nephritis

### Polycystic Kidney Disease (PKD)
- Autosomal dominant (ADPKD): most common inherited kidney disease; cysts enlarge and destroy kidney tissue over decades
- Extrarenal manifestations: liver cysts, intracranial aneurysms (Berry aneurysms)

### Renovascular Disease
- Renal artery stenosis from atherosclerosis or fibromuscular dysplasia
- Causes renovascular hypertension and ischemic nephropathy
- Signs: flash pulmonary edema, resistant hypertension, asymmetric kidney size

## CKD Staging by eGFR

| Stage | eGFR (mL/min/1.73m2) | Description |
|-------|---------------------|-------------|
| G1 | 90 or higher | Normal or high (kidney damage with normal function) |
| G2 | 60-89 | Mildly decreased |
| G3a | 45-59 | Mildly to moderately decreased |
| G3b | 30-44 | Moderately to severely decreased |
| G4 | 15-29 | Severely decreased |
| G5 | Less than 15 | Kidney failure (ESRD) |

Albuminuria categories (UACR):
- A1: Less than 30 mg/g (normal to mildly increased)
- A2: 30-300 mg/g (moderately increased / microalbuminuria)
- A3: Greater than 300 mg/g (severely increased / macroalbuminuria)

## Risk Factors

### Non-Modifiable
- Diabetes mellitus (Type 1 or 2) - most important risk factor
- Hypertension - second most important risk factor
- Older age (eGFR naturally declines with age)
- Family history of CKD, ESRD, or inherited nephropathy
- Race/ethnicity: African Americans, Hispanic Americans at higher risk (APOL1 gene variants)
- History of AKI
- Low birth weight / prematurity (fewer nephrons at birth)

### Modifiable
- Poorly controlled blood glucose and blood pressure
- Obesity and metabolic syndrome
- Smoking (reduces renal blood flow, promotes CKD progression)
- NSAID overuse (reduces renal perfusion, causes analgesic nephropathy)
- Contrast agents, aminoglycoside antibiotics, cisplatin (nephrotoxins)
- Recurrent kidney stones causing obstruction
- Recurrent urinary tract infections
- High-protein diet in established CKD

## Symptoms

### Early CKD (Stages 1-3, often silent)
- Often no symptoms; discovered incidentally on lab work
- Mild fatigue
- Foamy urine (proteinuria)
- Slightly elevated blood pressure
- Mild ankle swelling

### Moderate CKD (Stages 3-4)
- Increased fatigue and weakness
- Decreased urine output or nocturia (urinating at night)
- Persistent edema (face, ankles, abdomen)
- Anemia symptoms: pallor, fatigue, exertional dyspnea (erythropoietin deficiency)
- Worsening hypertension
- Pruritus (itching) from uremic toxin accumulation
- Bone disease (renal osteodystrophy): bone pain, fractures

### Advanced CKD / Uremia (Stage 5)
- Uremic symptoms: nausea, vomiting, anorexia, metallic taste in mouth
- Confusion, difficulty concentrating, encephalopathy
- Pericarditis: chest pain, pericardial friction rub
- Restless legs syndrome
- Muscle cramps, twitching
- Decreased urine output progressing to anuria

## Key Diagnostic Lab Values

### Kidney Function Tests
| Test | Normal | CKD Concern |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Serum Creatinine | 0.7-1.2 mg/dL (men); 0.5-1.1 (women) | Elevated; doubling creatinine = halving eGFR |
| eGFR (CKD-EPI formula) | 60 or higher mL/min/1.73m2 | Less than 60 for 3+ months = CKD |
| BUN (Blood Urea Nitrogen) | 7-20 mg/dL | Elevated in kidney disease |
| BUN:Creatinine ratio | 10:1 to 20:1 | Greater than 20:1 suggests pre-renal AKI |

### Urine Tests
| Test | Normal | Significance |
|------|--------|--------------|
| UACR (Urine albumin/creatinine) | Less than 30 mg/g | 30 or more = albuminuria; marker of kidney damage |
| Urine specific gravity | 1.005-1.030 | Fixed low = inability to concentrate (tubular dysfunction) |
| 24-hour urine protein | Less than 150 mg | Greater than 3.5 g = nephrotic range |

### Electrolytes and Metabolic Panel
- **Potassium**: Hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5 mEq/L) common in CKD; can cause fatal arrhythmias
- **Bicarbonate**: Metabolic acidosis (bicarbonate less than 22 mEq/L) in CKD
- **Phosphorus**: Elevated in CKD; causes vascular calcification and bone disease
- **Calcium**: Low (hypocalcemia) from reduced vitamin D activation
- **PTH**: Elevated (secondary hyperparathyroidism) from low calcium and high phosphate
- **Hemoglobin**: Normocytic normochromic anemia from EPO deficiency (target Hb 10-12 g/dL with treatment)
- **Vitamin D (25-OH)**: Often low in CKD

### Imaging
- **Renal ultrasound**: First-line; small echogenic kidneys in CKD; large kidneys in PKD, diabetes
- **Kidney biopsy**: Gold standard for glomerular disease diagnosis and staging

## Treatment

### Slowing CKD Progression
| Intervention | Target | Evidence |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Blood pressure control | Less than 130/80 mmHg | Prevents progression |
| Glycemic control in diabetes | HbA1c less than 7% | Reduces diabetic nephropathy |
| ACE inhibitors or ARBs | Reduce proteinuria | First-line with proteinuria; reduce progression |
| SGLT-2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) | Reduce CKD events | Reduce progression by ~40%; recommended for diabetic and non-diabetic CKD |
| Smoking cessation | Complete cessation | Slows progression |
| Protein restriction | 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day | In stages 3-5; reduces protein load on damaged kidneys |

### Renal Replacement Therapy (Stage 5 / ESRD)
- **Hemodialysis (HD)**: Most common modality; 3 sessions/week, 4 hours each; requires arteriovenous fistula
- **Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)**: Uses peritoneum as filter; done at home; continuous (CAPD) or automated (APD)
- **Kidney Transplantation**: Best long-term outcome; living-donor preferred; 5-year graft survival ~85%

### Dietary Recommendations in CKD
- **Potassium restriction**: 2-3 g/day in stages 3-5; avoid high-potassium foods (bananas, oranges, potatoes, tomatoes, dairy)
- **Phosphorus restriction**: 800-1000 mg/day; avoid processed foods, dark colas, nuts (in advanced CKD)
- **Protein restriction**: 0.6-0.8 g/kg/day in non-dialysis CKD; dialysis patients need 1.2-1.4 g/kg/day
- **Sodium restriction**: Less than 2 g/day to control blood pressure and fluid retention
- **Fluid restriction**: Most important in anuric ESRD patients on dialysis

### CKD Complications Management
- **Anemia**: Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) + IV or oral iron supplementation
- **Metabolic acidosis**: Sodium bicarbonate supplementation to keep bicarbonate 22 mEq/L or above
- **Secondary hyperparathyroidism**: Phosphate binders (sevelamer, calcium carbonate), active vitamin D (calcitriol), cinacalcet
- **Hyperkalemia**: Dietary restriction, loop diuretics, potassium binders (patiromer, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate)

## CKD Monitoring Schedule
- **Stages 1-2**: Annual labs (eGFR, UACR, electrolytes, blood pressure)
- **Stage 3**: Every 6 months
- **Stage 4**: Every 3 months
- **Stage 5 (pre-dialysis)**: Monthly labs

## When to Seek Nephrology Referral
- eGFR less than 30 mL/min/1.73m2 (Stage 4-5)
- UACR greater than 300 mg/g with declining eGFR
- Rapidly progressive kidney disease (more than 5 mL/min/year decline in eGFR)
- Unexplained proteinuria or hematuria requiring biopsy
- Hypertension refractory to 3 or more medications

## When to Seek Emergency Care
- Severe hyperkalemia: weakness, paralysis, arrhythmia, ECG changes
- Urine output less than 500 mL/day or complete anuria suddenly
- Severe fluid overload with breathing difficulty
- Severe metabolic acidosis (bicarbonate less than 10 mEq/L)
- Altered mental status or seizures from uremia
- Signs of pericarditis: pleuritic chest pain worse when lying down, friction rub
- Rapid sudden decline in kidney function
